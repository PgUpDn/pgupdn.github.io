Drawing Auxiliary Lines: When GNN Input Enrichment Helps Transformers Discover Newton

Created on April 17, 2026 Author: Xinyu Yang
AI Transformers Graph Neural Networks Inductive Bias World Models

Long-context Transformers trained on orbital trajectories often drift toward Keplerian curve fitting instead of Newtonian force reasoning. In this work, I tested three ways of injecting a GNN inductive bias and found that the simplest one, adding the GNN embedding directly to the input token, worked far better than cross-attention or output-side constraints.

TL;DR

Cross-attention left the GNN optional, so the Transformer ignored it. Output constraints only delivered marginal gains. Input enrichment, which I describe as drawing an auxiliary line, reduced the magnitude of the Keplerian bias by 58% and sharply improved Newtonian probe scores.

***

The Puzzle

A recent result from Liu et al. showed a striking phase transition in orbital world models: with short context, Transformers learn features aligned with Newtonian force; with long context, they default to Keplerian geometric descriptions. In historical terms, the model stops seeking laws and starts curve fitting.

That raises a simple question: if we already know the underlying physics depends on pairwise interaction, can we inject that structure into the model without completely redesigning the Transformer?

ΔN = average Newtonian probe score − average Keplerian probe score

In the manuscript, negative values of ΔN mean the representation is more Keplerian than Newtonian. The baseline model at context length 100 sits at -0.273, which is a clear bias toward Kepler-style structure.

***

The Core Idea: Draw an Auxiliary Line

In geometry, an auxiliary line does not add new truth to a problem. It makes an existing structure visible. That is the metaphor I use for the GNN in this project.

A GNN operating over the sun and planet nodes naturally performs the kind of message passing that exposes pairwise interaction. Instead of asking the Transformer to discover both the existence of that interaction and the law that governs it, we can let the GNN precompute a structured view and inject that view into the sequence model.

***

Three Integration Strategies

S1. Cross-attention

A frozen GNN acts as a side channel. The Transformer can consult it through gated cross-attention after each block.

S2. Physics-informed output

The output head is routed through a force bottleneck and a learned dynamics pathway, encouraging the model to use acceleration-like structure.

S3. Auxiliary line

The GNN embedding is added directly to the input token embedding, so it participates in every Q, K, and V computation from layer 0.

The difference is subtle but decisive. In S1 the GNN remains optional. In S3 it becomes part of the representation itself.

Strategy Best variant Result
Cross-attention S1-2n without gate Still ended at ΔN = -0.308. Other variants were even worse and often pushed the model deeper into Keplerian behavior.
Physics-informed output S2-rad Improved only marginally to around -0.239 to -0.249. Enough to help, not enough to change the underlying preference.

***

Why Cross-attention Failed

The baseline Transformer already has a low-loss shortcut: fit the long trajectory geometrically. Once that path is available, a gated cross-attention branch has no reason to stay open. The GNN becomes easy to ignore.

In the paper draft, this showed up directly in the numbers. Gated cross-attention made ΔN worse, and even the better ungated variant stayed close to baseline performance. The extra structure was present, but it never became foundational to the representation.

***

Why Input Enrichment Worked

  1. The GNN signal is fused into the token before the first attention layer, so there is no clean way for the Transformer to discard it.
  2. Message passing already expresses the core inductive bias: force comes from interaction between entities, not from isolated coordinate history.
  3. The richer 128-dimensional GNN embedding outperformed the bottleneck version, which suggests the model benefits from a diverse relational representation rather than a single scalar hint.

The broader lesson is that useful inductive bias may be less about forcing the model into a narrow pipeline and more about enriching what the model gets to see from the start.

***

What I Take Away From This

For scientific machine learning, representation engineering can matter more than architectural policing. If the right structure is exposed early enough, the model does not need to rediscover it from scratch.

That is why the auxiliary-line metaphor feels useful to me. The GNN is not solving the physics problem on behalf of the Transformer. It is simply drawing the line that makes the proof easier to see.
